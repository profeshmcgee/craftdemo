6 £200K Increased Kit Sales — Bolton Wanderers
~7-8 minutes

The Hesitation

So this is about Bolton Wanderers Football Club -- a professional football club in the UK. Bolton is actually one of the oldest living football clubs in the world, founded in 1874. And if you know anything about football culture over there, these clubs are deeply traditional -- some of them have been doing things the same way for over a hundred years. So when the idea came up to let fans vote on kit designs, leadership was genuinely nervous. No club had done anything like this before, and they were worried fans would troll the whole thing. If you remember the Boaty McBoatface situation -- where the public voted to name a research vessel and it went completely sideways -- that was exactly the kind of outcome they were afraid of. They needed confidence it would actually work before they committed.

Boaty McBoatface context (if anyone asks): In 2016, the UK's Natural Environment Research Council ran an online poll to name a new polar research ship. The internet overwhelmingly voted for "Boaty McBoatface." The organization ended up overriding the vote and naming the ship something else, which defeated the whole purpose. It became this cautionary tale about public engagement gone wrong -- exactly what Bolton was afraid of.

The Approach

Instead of pushing them straight into the kit vote, which was the high-stakes thing, I helped set up a smaller trial first -- matchday experience surveys. Low stakes, high engagement. Let fans weigh in on things like food, atmosphere, the overall experience. And what happened was fans took it seriously. The data was clean, the engagement was real, and once leadership could see that with their own eyes, they were ready to go bigger.
When it came time for the kit vote itself, I created a customized social survey through our platform -- designed specifically for this use case. It wasn't just a poll with three options. We built it so fans could see the kit designs, engage with them, and vote in a way that felt like they were actually part of the decision. The whole experience was branded to the club and embedded in their channels so it felt native, not like some third-party form they'd been sent to. That's what made it work -- it respected the fans and the tradition of the club at the same time.

The Result

The kit vote went live, fans loved it, and it drove over 200,000 pounds more in kit presales compared to the previous year. The board fully bought in, and the Marketing Lead who championed the whole thing ended up being promoted to Head of Communications for the club. That's what happens when you earn buy-in the right way -- everyone wins.

9 Client Discovery — Find Their True Pain Points
~6-7 minutes

Walk through each question with intention. Don't just read them -- explain why each one matters. The slide lists all five questions with their rationale.

So before I start building anything for a new client, I want to understand where they've been -- not just where they want to go. A lot of discovery calls are full of hypotheticals: "What would you do if...?" But I've found you get much better answers when you ask about past behaviors. What have they actually tried? What worked? What didn't? That tells me more about what this onboarding really needs than any forward-looking question ever could. So these five questions are designed to get real stories, not wishful thinking.

Question 1: What does success look like in 6 months?

This is always my first question because it gets past the surface. When someone says "we want to grow," that could mean a dozen things. Is it revenue? Retention? Brand awareness? All three? For GreenLeaf specifically, I'd want to understand -- is this about recovering abandoned carts, or is it about building a repeat customer base, or is it about getting people who buy once to come back for subscriptions? Each of those leads to a completely different email strategy. I need to know what we're actually building toward before I start recommending anything.

Question 2: Walk me through the current customer journey.

Before I build automation on top of anything, I want to understand the journey that already exists. Where are the gaps? Where are things working? What happens after someone places their first order -- do they hear from GreenLeaf again, or is it radio silence until the next promotion? You'd be surprised how often clients haven't actually mapped this out themselves, so doing it together in the kickoff becomes really valuable. It also surfaces things like "oh, we actually do send a thank-you email, but it's manual and only happens sometimes." Those are the quick fixes that make a big difference.

Question 3: What's been tried before that didn't stick?

This is the question that tells me where the skepticism is really coming from. If the Head of Commerce had a bad experience with a previous tool, or if the team tried to set up automations before and it fell apart, I need to know that. Because I'm going to run into that same wall if I don't understand what's behind it. And this is also where past behavior tells me more than hypotheticals -- if someone says "we tried Klaviyo and nobody used it after the first month," that's a huge signal about what this onboarding actually needs to prioritize.

Question 4: Who's in Mailchimp day-to-day, and how comfortable are they?

This one's practical. If I don't right-size the training, I'll either bore someone who's already comfortable or completely overwhelm someone who's never been in the platform. And with a small team that's already stretched thin, overwhelming them is the fastest way to lose them. I also want to know who the internal champion is -- the person who's actually going to log in and use the tool. That might not be the person who signed the contract. Knowing who that is changes how I structure the whole onboarding.

Question 5: What would make the Head of Commerce say "this was worth it" in 90 days?

This is the one that ties it all together. It gives us a concrete win condition -- something we can actually measure -- and then we build the entire roadmap around getting there. I'm asking the team to put a specific answer on this, not a vague one. "We want more sales" isn't a win condition. "We want to recover 15% of abandoned carts and see a 20% open rate on our welcome series" -- that's something I can build a plan around and prove. If I don't have this answer, I'm guessing at what matters. And guessing is how things go sideways.

Transition: "So once I have those answers, I've got enough to build a real plan. Let me walk you through what the first 90 days would look like -- broken into three phases."

10 First 30 Days — Get the Foundation Right
~4-5 minutes

The slide has two columns: Technical Setup and Quick Wins, both numbered 1-3 with the GreenLeaf logo. Walk through each column.

Technical Setup

The first 30 days are about getting the foundation right. On the technical side, we're connecting Shopify so customer and order data syncs automatically -- no more manual work. Mailchimp's Shopify integration pulls in purchase history, product data, and customer profiles, which means we can start segmenting from day one instead of building lists from scratch.
We're cleaning that contact list, because sending to 10,000 unverified contacts is a recipe for deliverability problems. This means running the list through Mailchimp's built-in verification, removing hard bounces and obviously inactive addresses, and identifying who's actually engaged versus who hasn't opened anything in a year. And we're setting up tagging and segments so we actually know who our customers are -- first-time buyers, repeat customers, cart abandoners, high-value purchasers -- before we send them anything.

Quick Wins

On the quick wins side -- and this is important -- we're getting a Welcome email series and Abandoned Cart emails live as early as possible. The Welcome series is usually 3-4 emails over the first week: a warm hello with the brand story, a product highlight or bestseller showcase, maybe a first-purchase incentive. The Abandoned Cart flow triggers automatically when someone leaves items behind -- a gentle reminder at one hour, a follow-up at 24 hours, maybe a final nudge at 72 with social proof or a small discount.
These are the things that start recovering revenue almost immediately, and they're the kind of early wins that build confidence with the whole team. The POC sees results, the Head of Commerce sees revenue, and nobody had to do anything too complicated to get there. We also get the team a hands-on walkthrough so they're comfortable in the tool and not feeling like this is something being done to them -- I want them to feel ownership, not dependency.

11 First 60 Days — Show It's Working
~4-5 minutes

Same two-column layout. This phase is about proving value and optimizing.

Optimization

Phase two is about proving it. We look at what's performing and start tweaking -- timing, subject lines, content. Are the cart recovery emails converting better at one hour or three hours? Is the welcome series drop-off happening after email two or email three? We A/B test the things that matter and leave alone what's already working.
We build audience segments so the right message is hitting the right customer -- someone who's bought three times shouldn't be getting the same email as a first-time visitor. This is where Mailchimp's predictive segmentation is really powerful, especially for e-commerce. We can target based on predicted lifetime value, purchase likelihood, even lapsed customers who are at risk of churning. And we set up a post-purchase sequence to turn first-time buyers into repeat customers -- a thank-you, a product care tip, a cross-sell recommendation based on what they bought.

Proving It to the Skeptic

And this is where the skeptic becomes important. I'm pulling actual cart recovery revenue and putting it in front of the Head of Commerce. Not open rates, not click rates -- revenue. Money that came back because of something we built. That's the language that lands. And by this point, the cart and welcome sequences should be performing stronger in week two than week one -- that upward trend is a powerful thing to be able to show someone who wasn't sold at the start.

14 Hold Your Fire. Stay the Course.
~5-6 minutes

Two columns: "Clean List First" and "Support the Team." Be honest here -- this is where your real experience shows. You've actually lived through both of these.

Clean List First

The first thing I'd be watching for is the temptation to start sending before we've cleaned that list. They've got 10,000 contacts that haven't been touched in a year. If we blast all of them right away, bounces and spam complaints are going to damage their sender reputation, and that is genuinely hard to come back from.
I've lived through this one. At Social Asking, early on, we sent through SendGrid to an unclean list and our emails started landing in spam. I had to rebuild our reputation with careful segmentation, slow drip sends, and honestly just time. It was a painful lesson, but it's one I never had to learn twice. So with GreenLeaf, we validate the list before anything goes out. We start with the most engaged contacts, and we warm up the domain gradually. Trust is harder to rebuild than it is to protect.

Support the Team

The second thing I'd watch for is adoption dropping off. Their team is already stretched thin. If Mailchimp starts feeling like one more thing on an already full to-do list, they'll stop logging in and everything we set up just sits there.
I saw a version of this at Brain Bytes. Some of our marketing clients got overwhelmed by the scope of their retainer. They'd signed up for this big package and then just... froze. I learned to scale back, focus on what they could actually absorb, and layer in more once they had their footing.

Concrete Mitigations

So here's what I'd actually put in place from day one. First, I'd co-create a launch plan with the team during kickoff -- not hand them one, build it together. That way everyone knows what's coming, when, and what their part is. When people have a checklist they helped write, they're way more likely to follow through.
Second, I'd establish a regular cadence -- not just milestone reviews at 30, 60, 90, but weekly or biweekly check-ins, especially in the first month. Short, focused, and always tied to something concrete. "Here's what we did, here's what it produced, here's what's next." That rhythm keeps the team engaged and keeps me close enough to catch problems early.
And third -- and this one matters a lot -- I'd make myself available to them in chat whenever they have time. Not just scheduled calls. If someone on the team hits a wall at 2pm on a Tuesday and they can ping me and get unblocked in five minutes instead of waiting for our next meeting, that's the difference between them staying in the tool and giving up on it. I want to remove every excuse not to use it.
Celebrate early wins loudly. Remind the team of the 90-day win condition we set together. And remember: one bad campaign isn't a failed strategy. Adoption doesn't die at the start. It dies in the middle, when nobody's paying attention. That's where I stay closest.
